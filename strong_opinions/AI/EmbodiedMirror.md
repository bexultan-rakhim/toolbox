# The Mirror Problem: Why LLMs Look More Intelligent Than They Are

## Opinion

Ask a monkey to use an LLM and you will get outputs as intelligent as a monkey. That is not a joke about monkeys. It is a precise observation about where the intelligence in the human-LLM interaction actually comes from. When a skilled engineer gets a remarkable output from a language model, the instinct is to attribute the remarkableness to the model. I think that attribution is wrong, and I think the reason most people cannot see that it is wrong is that they are unfamiliar with embodied cognitive science — a field that has spent decades carefully studying what intelligence actually is, where it lives, and how it emerges. If that research were better known, I believe the current excitement around LLMs would look considerably different.

My claim is not that LLMs are useless or that interacting with them produces nothing of value. I think they are genuinely remarkable tools and a novel form of cognitive extension — more participatory than anything we have built before. My claim is more specific: LLMs are mirrors. They reflect human intelligence back in a form that feels generative, and the feedback loop between a capable human and a language model is so fluent and so fast that it becomes very difficult to see which side of the loop the intelligence is actually coming from. I think we are systematically misattributing it, and I think that misattribution is costing us — in bad claims, bad investment decisions, and a failure to seriously pursue research directions that would get us much further.

---

## Rationale

I think this argument rests on three claims that I believe deserve equal weight.

**First: the mirror effect is real, and the feedback loop explains most of what feels remarkable about LLMs.** LLMs are trained on the outputs of human cognition — text produced by beings who have bodies, histories, sensorimotor experience, and situated understanding of the world. When a language model produces something that feels intelligent, it is drawing on the statistical residue of all of that. The intelligence was deposited into the training data by humans. The model learned to reproduce its shape. What the interaction returns to you is a reflection of human cognition, reorganized and fed back through a system that is extraordinarily good at producing fluent, contextually appropriate language.

The human is the variable that explains output quality. A domain expert who asks precise, well-framed questions with rich context gets outputs that look expert-level. A novice who asks vague questions with thin context gets outputs that look thin. The model did not become more intelligent for the expert. The expert brought more intelligence into the loop, and got more back. This is what I mean by a mirror — not that the reflection is perfect or passive, but that the quality of what comes back is primarily a function of what went in.

I also think this explains something that is not discussed enough: why junior developers are struggling with LLMs in ways that are starting to show up in codebases and teams. A junior developer using an LLM before they have built a genuine mental model of the domain cannot evaluate what they get back. They cannot tell a good output from a plausible-looking bad one, because they have not yet developed the native fluency that would let them drive the interaction intelligently. The feedback loop requires a capable human on one end to function well. This is not an argument against junior developers using LLMs — I think LLMs can accelerate learning dramatically for someone who knows how to learn. But learning is itself a skill, and a tool that produces fluent output on demand can mask the absence of understanding in ways that older, less forgiving tools could not.

The flip side of this is equally important, and I think it is discussed even less: senior engineers are not immune to the mirror effect — they are arguably more susceptible to it. A skilled engineer brings so much into the interaction that the output genuinely looks remarkable, and the temptation to attribute that remarkableness to the model rather than to themselves is real and well documented. Research from Aalto University found a reversal of the Dunning-Kruger effect with LLMs — higher AI literacy brought more overconfidence, not less, with users who considered themselves more AI literate assuming their abilities were greater than they actually were [2]. The mirror is more flattering to someone who is already good. A senior engineer gets back something that looks like senior engineering, and it takes deliberate effort to notice that they brought most of it with them. This is not a failure of intelligence. It is a predictable consequence of interacting with a system that reflects your own competence back at you fluently and at speed.

**Second: embodied cognitive science tells us something precise about what LLMs are missing, and I think it is the most important thing missing.** The field has been building a serious scientific account of intelligence for decades, and the core finding is that intelligence is not a disembodied symbol manipulation process. It is grounded in a body, in physical interaction with an environment, in sensorimotor feedback loops that develop over time through situated experience. Merleau-Ponty argued that perception and cognition cannot be separated from the body that enacts them [1]. Varela, Thompson and Rosch extended this into a full research program — enactivism — arguing that cognition is not representation of a pre-given world but the enactment of a world through embodied action [2]. More recently, Alva Noë has argued that perception itself is something we do, not something that happens to us, and that understanding requires the kind of engagement with an environment that disembodied systems simply cannot have [3].

Consider termite mounds. If you encounter one without knowing anything about termites, the structure looks purposeful — sophisticated ventilation shafts, thermal regulation, structural load distribution across the walls. It looks like something designed by an engineer who understood the problem. The reality is that no individual termite has any understanding of the whole. Each termite responds only to local conditions — pheromone gradients, the presence of other termites, the immediate physical environment — and deposits signals that trigger responses in others. Global structure emerges from these locally driven individual behaviours with no central coordination and no termite that holds the plan. This process is called stigmergy [5]. Crucially, the environment is not a passive recipient of the construction — wind, rain and erosion participate actively, shaping the structure during construction by removing what cannot survive the conditions and reinforcing what can [5]. The mound that stands is not the one a smart termite designed. It is the one that the interaction between simple rules and environmental pressure produced over time.

The optimality of the structure is real. The intelligence implied by that optimality is not. This is the distinction I think applies directly to LLMs.

I want to be careful here because I pushed back on the simple version of this claim earlier. LLMs are not tools in the passive sense — they participate in cognitive loops in ways that notebooks and calculators do not. Clark and Chalmers' extended mind thesis is the right frame: cognition extends beyond the skull into tools, environments, and other agents, and an LLM with tool use and real-time human interaction is genuinely part of an extended cognitive system [4]. I think that is true and important. But being part of an extended cognitive system is not the same as being a cognitive agent. The termite mound is part of a remarkable system too.

I think LLMs are producing something structurally similar. The fluent, contextually appropriate outputs are not evidence of understanding. They are the result of statistical patterns over an enormous corpus of human-generated text, interacting with the structure of a capable human's input, producing something that looks like intelligent response. The appearance of design does not require a designer. The appearance of understanding does not require understanding.

What LLMs specifically lack — and what embodied cognitive science identifies as load-bearing for genuine intelligence — is the developmental, sensorimotor, situated kind of grounding that Varela and Merleau-Ponty describe. The kind that comes from having a body that moves through a world, that gets things wrong and feels the consequences, that builds understanding through interaction rather than through pattern matching over descriptions of interaction. This is not a claim that LLMs will never have anything like this. It is a claim about what they currently are and what the current excitement is actually responding to.

**Third: we have better research foundations available, and I think they are being seriously underinvested.** The embodied cognition research program is not speculative. It spans decades, involves serious empirical work across neuroscience, cognitive science, robotics and philosophy, and has produced a substantial body of findings about what intelligence requires and how it emerges. Embodied AI — systems designed to perceive, act, learn and develop through interaction with real environments — represents a research direction grounded in that science, one that takes seriously the findings about sensorimotor grounding, situatedness, and developmental learning that the LLM paradigm largely bypasses [6].

I am not claiming that embodied AI has solved the problems LLMs have not. It has not, and the challenges are significant. What I am claiming is that the research foundations exist, the scientific case is serious, and the investment and attention it receives is not commensurate with that seriousness. The current concentration of resources on LLMs is not just a market preference — it is actively shaping what gets funded, what gets staffed, and what gets treated as the legitimate frontier of the field. I think that concentration is making it harder to reach the point of disillusionment that would redirect attention toward approaches with stronger scientific grounding. The mirror is too flattering. It keeps reflecting back something that looks like progress.
